\section{Hoare Logic and Its Scalability Problem}

\subsubsection*{Overview of Hoare Logic}
Hoare logic is a reasoning system for proving the specifications of programs.
It uses Hoare triples of the form $\{P\} C \{Q\}$ to describe program
specifications, where $C$ is the program, $P$ and $Q$ are assertions that
describe the precondition and postcondition about program states respectively.
A Hoare triple is true if, whenever the precondition $P$ holds before executing
the program $C$, the postcondition $Q$ will hold after executing $C$.
The Hoare logic provides rules for how to derive valid Hoare triples based on
the structure of the program.

For example, the following Hoare triple describes a simple assignment:

$$
\{ x = 0 \}\ x := x + 1\ \{ x = 1 \}
$$

It says that if $x$ is $0$ before executing the assignment $x := x + 1$,
then $x$ is $1$ afterward.

\subsubsection*{Separation Matters in Program Verification}
For programs that manipulate heap-allocated data structures (like linked lists),
awareness of difference heap locations (pointers) is crucial for reasoning about
program behavior.
For example, if we want to reason about a list deallocation program:
\begin{figure}[H]
  
\begin{lstlisting}
LDispose(x) {
  while (x != null) {
    y := x;
    x := [x + 1];
    dispose(y);
    dispose(y + 1)
  };
  return null
}

\end{lstlisting}
\caption{List Dispose}
\end{figure}

For this program, the Hoare triple we can prove is:

$$
\vdash \{ \List(x) \} \LDispose(x) \{ x = null \}
$$

which means if $x$ points to a valid list before executing $\LDispose$, then
after executing $\LDispose$, $x$ will be null.

However, Hoare logic has scalability issues in a sense that it does not provide
a clear and simple way to extend already proven specifications with richer
assertions.

For example, suppose we have proved the Hoare triple: $\vdash \{ \List(x) \}
\LDispose(x) \{ x = null \}$. 
How can we apply it to conclude the postcondition after executing $\LDispose$ in
a program state that contains two lists $x$ and $y$: $\List(x) \land \List(y)$.

With a simple glance, the precondition is $\List(x) \land \List(y)$, and the
postcondition should be $x = null \land \List(y)$.
But this is not true, because $x$ and $y$ could point to the same list! 
So what we can at most conclude is $x = null$, but we do not know whether $y$ is
still a valid list.

\subsubsection*{Add Separation Assertions to the Specification}
To make the specification describe the program correctly and precisely, we need
to use a more complex assertion that describes the separation of the two lists
in the precondition:

$$
(\List(x) \land \List(y)) \land
(\forall u.\ \reach(x, u) \land \reach(y, u) \Rightarrow u = null)
$$

The $\forall u.\ \reach(x, u) \land \reach(y, u) \Rightarrow u = null$ part of
the assertion says that the two lists $x$ and $y$ are separated, i.e., they do
not share any common nodes.

Then we can possibly conclude the postcondition $x = null \land \List(y)$.

If we have three lists $x$, $y$ and $z$, we need to write precondition like:

$$
\begin{array}{l}
(\List(x, \alpha) \land \List(y, \beta)) \\
\land\;(\forall u.\ \reach(x, u) \land \reach(y, u) \Rightarrow u = null) \\
\land\;\List(z) \\
\land\;(\forall u.\ \reach(z, u) \land (\reach(x, u) \lor \reach(y, u))
  \Rightarrow u = null)
\end{array}
$$

And the postcondition is $x = null \land \List(y) \land \List(z) \land 
(\forall u.\ \reach(z, u) \land \reach(y, u) \Rightarrow u = null)$.


It does not scale:
\begin{itemize}
  \item It is not even clear where to add the separation assertion.
  \item When we add more separation assertions, the complexity of formulas grows
  quadratically. Therefore the time needed to solve the formulas also grows fast.
\end{itemize}

\section{Separation Logic}
The scalability problem of Hoare logic is caused by the fact that the formulas
in the Hoare triples always describe the \emph{entire} program heap.
Hence, when we want to extend the specification to describe more states, we must
write more complex formulas that assert the separation of added states with the
existing states.
Separation logic is a an approach that addresses this problem directly by
introducing a new logical connective called the \textit{separating conjunction},
written as $\star$.
Instead of describing the entire heap, a formula in separation logic only
describes only a \emph{part} of the heap, and it uses $\star$ to combine two
formulas that describe two strictly separate parts of the heap.
With this separating connective, we can extend the specification in a
compositional way, without needing to write extra formulas that assert the
separations.

\subsubsection*{Syntax of Separation Logic Assertions}
\textbf{Assertions.}
The set of \emph{assertions}, $\Assert$, ranged over by
$P, P_1, Q, Q_1, \ldots$, is defined by:

\[
\begin{array}{rcl@{\qquad}l}
P \in \Assert & ::= &
P \land P \mid P \Rightarrow P \mid \True \mid \False
& \text{classical connectives} \\
& \mid &
E = E \mid E > E \mid E \in X \mid \cdots
& \text{boolean assertions} \\
& \mid &
\exists x.\ P
& \text{quantification} \\
& \mid &
P \star P \mid P \sepimp P \mid \Emp
\mid \displaystyle\mathop{\circledast}_{E_1 \le x < E_2} P
& \text{separating connectives} \\
& \mid &
E \mapsto E
& \text{cell assertion}
\end{array}
\]

\subsubsection*{Meaning of Separation Logic Assertions}
What kind of program states does an assertion describe?
The meaning of the main assertion forms is given by the satisfaction relation
$e, s, h \models P$:

{\small
\[
\begin{array}{rcl}
e, s, h \models P_1 \land P_2
& \Longleftrightarrow &
e, s, h \models P_1 \land e, s, h \models P_2 \\
e, s, h \models P_1 \Rightarrow P_2
& \Longleftrightarrow &
e, s, h \models P_1 \Rightarrow e, s, h \models P_2 \\
e, s, h \models \True
& \Longleftrightarrow &
\text{always} \\
e, s, h \models \False
& \Longleftrightarrow &
\text{never} \\
e, s, h \models E_1 = E_2
& \Longleftrightarrow &
(\Eval{E_1 = E_2} = \true) \land h = \emptyset \\
e, s, h \models E_1 > E_2
& \Longleftrightarrow &
(\Eval{E_1 > E_2} = \true) \land h = \emptyset \\
e, s, h \models E \in X
& \Longleftrightarrow &
(\Eval{E \in X} = \true) \land h = \emptyset \\
e, s, h \models \exists x.\ P
& \Longleftrightarrow &
\exists v \in \Val.\ e[x \mapsto v], s, h \models P
\end{array}
\]
}

{\small\color{blue}
\[
\begin{array}{@{}rcl@{}}
e, s, h \models P_1 \star P_2
& \Longleftrightarrow &
\begin{aligned}[t]
& \exists h_1, h_2 \in \Heap.\ h = h_1 \uplus h_2 \land \\
& e, s, h_1 \models P_1 \land e, s, h_2 \models P_2
\end{aligned} \\
e, s, h \models \Emp
& \Longleftrightarrow &
h = \emptyset \\
e, s, h \models \displaystyle\mathop{\circledast}_{E_1 \le x < E_2} P
& \Longleftrightarrow &
\begin{aligned}[t]
& \Eval{E_1} = i \in \mathbb{N}
  \land \Eval{E_2} = k \in \mathbb{N} \land \\
& \bigl(i < k \Rightarrow
  \exists h_i, \ldots, h_{k-1}.\ h = h_i \uplus \cdots \uplus h_{k-1}
  \land \\
& \qquad \forall j.\ i \le j < k.\ e, s, h_j \models P[j/x]\bigr) \land \\
& \bigl(i \ge k \Rightarrow h = \emptyset\bigr)
\end{aligned} \\
e, s, h \models E_1 \mapsto E_2
& \Longleftrightarrow &
h = \{\Eval{E_1} \mapsto \Eval{E_2}\} \\
e, s, h \models \Pred(\vec{E})
& \Longleftrightarrow &
\cdots
\end{array}
\]
}

\subsubsection*{Separation Logic Rules}
Some core proof rules for the language without functions are:

{\scriptsize
\[
\begin{array}{@{}c@{\quad}l@{}}
\dfrac{x \notin \pv(E')}
      {\triple{x = E' \star E \in \Val}{x := E}{x = E[E'/x]}}
& \textsc{assign} \\[4ex]

\dfrac{x \notin \pv(E')}
      {\triple{x = E' \star E \mapsto E_1}
              {x := [E]}
              {x = E_1[E'/x] \star E[E'/x] \mapsto E_1[E'/x]}}
& \textsc{lookup} \\[4ex]

\dfrac{}
      {\triple{E_1 \mapsto E \star E_2 \in \Val}
              {[E_1] := E_2}
              {E_1 \mapsto E_2}}
& \textsc{mutate} \\[4ex]

\dfrac{x \notin \pv(E')}
      {\triple{x = E' \star E \in \mathbb{N}}
              {x := \mathsf{new}(E)}
              {\exists y.\ x = y \star
               \mathop{\circledast}_{0 \le i < E[E'/x]}((y + i) \mapsto \Null)}}
& \textsc{new} \\[4ex]

\dfrac{}
      {\triple{E \mapsto E_1}{\mathsf{dispose}(E)}{\Emp}}
& \textsc{dispose} \\[4ex]

\dfrac{}
      {\triple{\Emp}{\mathsf{skip}}{\Emp}}
& \textsc{skip} \\[4ex]

\dfrac{\triple{P}{C_1}{Q} \qquad \triple{Q}{C_2}{R}}
      {\triple{P}{C_1;\ C_2}{R}}
& \textsc{seq} \\[4ex]

\dfrac{\triple{P \land E}{C_1}{Q_1}
       \qquad
       \triple{P \land \neg E}{C_2}{Q_2}}
      {\triple{P \star E \in \Bool}
              {\mathsf{if}\ (E)\ C_1\ \mathsf{else}\ C_2}
              {Q_1 \lor Q_2}}
& \textsc{if} \\[4ex]

\dfrac{\triple{P \land E}{C}{P \star E \in \Bool}}
      {\triple{P \star E \in \Bool}
              {\mathsf{while}\ (E)\ C}
              {P \land \neg E}}
& \textsc{while} \\[4ex]

\dfrac{\triple{P}{C}{Q}
       \qquad
       \Mod(C) \cap \pv(R) = \emptyset}
      {\triple{P \star R}{C}{Q \star R}}
& \textsc{frame} \\[4ex]

\dfrac{\models \blueassn{P \Rightarrow P'}
       \qquad
       \triple{P'}{C}{Q'}
       \qquad
       \models \blueassn{Q' \Rightarrow Q}}
      {\triple{P}{C}{Q}}
& \textsc{cons} \\[4ex]

\dfrac{\triple{P}{C}{Q}}
      {\triple{\exists x.\ P}{C}{\exists x.\ Q}}
& \textsc{exists} \\[4ex]

\dfrac{\triple{P_1}{C}{Q} \qquad \triple{P_2}{C}{Q}}
      {\triple{P_1 \lor P_2}{C}{Q}}
& \textsc{disj}
\end{array}
\]
}

\subsubsection*{Soundness of Separation Logic}
\[
\vdash \{P\} C \{Q\} \Rightarrow \models \{P\} C \{Q\}
\]
